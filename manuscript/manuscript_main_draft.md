# MOEA-FIND: A Multi-Objective Optimization Framework for Structured Synthetic Drought Ensemble Generation

## Key Points

1. A single Borg MOEA optimization run, coupled to the Kirsch-Nowak generator through a Manhattan-distance auxiliary objective, produces an ensemble of synthetic streamflow traces whose drought characteristics span the physically achievable region of a user-chosen drought signature space.
2. Adding a Manhattan distance to a user-supplied anti-ideal point as a $(K+1)$-th objective makes every feasible trace mutually non-dominated, so that the epsilon-dominance archive of Borg MOEA tiles the feasible drought characteristic region along the $K$ target axes.
3. A dimension sweep from $K=2$ to $K=6$ on a constrained analytic benchmark confirms interior-filling coverage, and the method is demonstrated on a single-site Cannonsville application with a closing scenario discovery exercise performed directly in drought hazard space.

## Plain Language Summary

Water supply planners evaluate reservoir operating policies and drought management rules by running simulations over ensembles of synthetic streamflow records. The conclusions that can be drawn about policy robustness depend on how thoroughly those ensembles span the range of drought severities, durations, and seasonal patterns that matter for the basin at hand. Search-based generators in the published literature produce one synthetic trace per optimization run, each matched to a user-specified drought signature, so that building an ensemble requires the analyst to specify a grid of target signatures in advance without knowing which combinations are physically achievable from the historical record and the underlying generator. Library subsampling methods draw from a pre-generated bank of traces using criteria defined in the forcing variable space rather than in the drought hazard space that planners ultimately care about.

This paper presents MOEA-FIND, a method that produces an ensemble of synthetic streamflow traces spanning the range of drought signatures achievable from the historical record in a single optimization run rather than one trace per user-specified target. Each candidate solution is a synthetic streamflow trace, and a single auxiliary Manhattan-distance objective added alongside the drought characteristics causes every feasible trace to become Pareto-optimal under the multi-objective formulation. The epsilon-dominance archive of the Borg multi-objective evolutionary algorithm then tiles the set of feasible drought signatures at a user-specified resolution, and the archive is the deliverable ensemble. The method is validated on analytic test problems in two to six target dimensions and demonstrated on the Cannonsville inflow with three custom drought characteristics including a cyclic peak timing feature. The method can be applied to any drought feature for which a distance to an undesirable value can be computed, including seasonal timing features, so planners can adapt it to basin-specific concerns.

## Abstract

Robust evaluation of reservoir operating policies and drought management rules under deep uncertainty requires synthetic streamflow ensembles that span the range of physically plausible drought hazards. Existing search-based drought generators formulate ensemble construction as a target-matching problem in which simulated annealing minimizes a weighted-sum deviation from a user-specified vector of drought characteristics, each optimization run produces one synthetic trace, and building an ensemble requires the analyst to supply a grid of target signatures whose attainability against the underlying generator is not known in advance. Library-and-subsample alternatives draw from a pre-generated bank of traces but operate in the forcing-variable space rather than in the drought hazard space that planners evaluate, so coverage of the latter is incidental. This paper introduces MOEA-FIND, a method that reformulates drought ensemble generation as a multi-objective evolutionary optimization problem coupling the Borg MOEA to the Kirsch-Nowak nonparametric streamflow generator. A single auxiliary Manhattan-distance objective added alongside $K$ target drought characteristics causes every feasible trace to become Pareto-optimal under the resulting $K+1$-objective problem, so that the epsilon-dominance archive of Borg MOEA tiles the full $K$-dimensional feasible drought hazard region along the $K$ orthogonal target axes. An analytic dimension sweep from $K=2$ to $K=6$ on a constrained benchmark confirms that the induced coverage statistically matches uniform sampling over the same region on mean distance from the anti-ideal, interior mass fraction, signed orthant occupancy, and coarse grid cell occupancy. The method is demonstrated on the Cannonsville inflow with three custom drought characteristics including a cyclic peak timing feature, and its value for downstream vulnerability analysis is illustrated through a scenario discovery exercise conducted directly in drought hazard space using gradient boosted tree classification. The method is evaluated against equal-size random and Latin hypercube subsamples of a Kirsch-Nowak library in a feasible-region coverage comparison that forms the empirical test of the reformulation.

## 1. Introduction

Robust decision making under deep uncertainty provides the standard framework for evaluating water supply operating policies against conditions that lie outside the observed record (Lempert et al., 2003; Kasprzyk et al., 2013; Herman et al., 2015). The many-objective robust decision making extension of this framework, hereafter MORDM (Kasprzyk et al., 2013), simulates each candidate policy across an ensemble of plausible future stressors and quantifies robustness through the distribution of system performance across that ensemble (Hadjimichael et al., 2020). For water supply systems whose binding risk is meteorological drought, the stressor ensemble is a collection of synthetic streamflow records, and the conclusions that a planner can draw about policy robustness are bounded by how thoroughly that ensemble spans the drought hazard space of interest. The drought hazard space is defined by the drought characteristics that enter the analyst's robustness metric, typically mean severity, event duration, peak intensity, seasonal timing, and any system-specific threshold crossings that condition reservoir operating rules for the basin under study.

The methodological landscape for synthetic streamflow generation is mature at the level of forcing variables. Parametric autoregressive models and their seasonal extensions reproduce monthly and annual first- and second-order statistics of the observed record. Nonparametric block-bootstrap methods originating with Kirsch et al. (2013) and extended to multi-site disaggregation by Nowak et al. (2010) preserve the historical flow duration curve, the seasonal cycle, and the cross-site correlation structure by resampling from the historical record with a Cholesky factorisation of the monthly correlation matrix. Weighted bootstrap extensions (Herman et al., 2016) adjust the resampling weights so that the lowest quantiles of the historical distribution are emphasised, producing ensembles with a controlled increase in drought frequency while retaining the historical seasonal and spatial structure. Parametric generators with kappa-distribution marginals and vine-copula dependence structures (Svensson et al., 2017; Brechmann et al., 2017) extend the envelope of synthesised drought signatures beyond the historical range at the cost of additional parametric assumptions. Phase-randomisation and frequency-domain methods (Papalexiou, 2019) provide a third alternative that preserves spectral properties while allowing parametric tail extension. In each of these generator families the user controls the sample distribution in the forcing-variable space of the generator rather than in the drought hazard space that the downstream analysis evaluates, and the question of whether a given ensemble covers the relevant drought hazard space receives comparatively little attention. Herman et al. (2020) identify the construction of synthetic scenarios whose statistical properties are informed by specific climate processes and model errors as a distinct open research gap in the climate adaptation literature, and Bonham et al. (2025) note that ensembles derived from climate projections systematically underrepresent challenging conditions relative to moderate ones because the underlying input distributions are not calibrated against the downstream performance space.

Three prior papers reformulate drought ensemble generation as an optimization problem and are the direct methodological predecessors of the present work. Borgomeo et al. (2015) introduced a simulated annealing procedure that permutes a bootstrapped monthly record until a weighted-sum deviation from a user-supplied target vector of historical statistics is minimised, producing one synthetic trace per optimization run. The decision vector is a permutation of bootstrapped monthly flows, the target vector contains means, monthly standard deviations, monthly and interannual autocorrelations, and the output is a single trace that matches the target. Representative ensembles in that work are produced by re-running the optimizer against the same target with different random initial bootstraps, so the resulting ensemble reflects stochastic variation at a fixed target rather than coverage of alternative targets. Borgomeo et al. (2015) explicitly identify the extension of the formulation to multi-objective search as an open direction for future work, and the present paper is a response to that direction.

Zaniolo et al. (2024) adapted the Borgomeo procedure to drought-specific targeting through the FIND method. FIND defines three drought characteristics on the Standardized Streamflow Index (frequency, intensity, and duration), allows the user to specify the target values of each as either absolute or as fractional changes relative to historical, and minimises a weighted sum of deviation terms against that target triple. The operator swaps replacement values drawn from a historical n-month cumulative distribution function for monthly values in the synthetic record. The method produces one synthetic trace per target triple, and an ensemble spanning a rectangular grid of target combinations is produced by running the optimizer once per grid cell. The authors demonstrate this by enumerating a regular grid of drought duration and intensity targets and running the full optimization procedure multiple times at each grid cell, and the achievable subset of that grid is evaluated ex post. The weights in the objective function must be retuned when the target regime moves away from historical conditions, which is an implicit admission that single-objective target matching does not scale gracefully across the drought characteristic plane.

Wheeler et al. (2025) extended the Borgomeo (2015) construction to multi-site basins. The extension expands the target vector with cross-site correlation matrices and modifies the swap operator to act on a single randomly chosen site per iteration, preserving the single-objective weighted-sum formulation. Each optimization run produces one joint multisite record that matches one high-dimensional target vector containing per-site means, monthly standard deviations, monthly and interannual autocorrelations, a Hurst coefficient for long-range dependence, and cross-site correlation matrices. The climate-change demonstration in that work scales target Hurst coefficients by a single perturbation factor and leaves broader exploration of climatic covariates to future work. Across all three predecessors the common limitation is structural: each run produces one trace that matches one pre-specified target, the fraction of the drought hazard space that is physically achievable from the historical record and the underlying generator is never mapped directly, and ensembles are assembled by enumerating target vectors that the analyst must supply in advance.

A second line of work builds ensembles by library and subsample. Bonham et al. (2024) apply conditioned Latin hypercube sampling to a pre-generated library of streamflow, demand, and initial storage scenarios in the Colorado River Basin, and evaluate the quality of the subsample using space-filling metrics computed in the uncertainty input coordinates of the generator. They demonstrate that input-space space-filling metrics are strong predictors of robustness ranking preservation across the subsample size range they test, but the entire analysis is conducted in the coordinates of the input factors rather than in the coordinates of the drought hazard features that planners evaluate. Hadjimichael et al. (2020) sample a parameter space of hidden Markov model hyperparameters and human-system multipliers with a Latin hypercube design and generate multiple synthetic streamflow realisations per sampled state of the world, producing an ensemble whose coverage in the outcome space is an emergent property of the upstream sample and the generator nonlinearity. Lau et al. (2023) define safe operating spaces as regions in decision-variable space within which adaptive policy pathways retain acceptable performance, and sample the uncertainty space to populate those regions, again operating on the input side of the generator map. Moallemi et al. (2020) characterise the exploratory modelling tradition explicitly in terms of three distinct spaces (a decision space of candidate policies, an uncertainty space of states of the world, and an outcome space of performance measures), and the standard workflow samples the first two and measures the third. Quinn et al. (2020) show that analyses framed as scenario-neutral are not in fact neutral, because the choice of input sampling distribution conditions the conclusions drawn from the vulnerability analysis. Across this literature the sampling is conducted in a space upstream of the drought hazard region, and coverage of the hazard region is left as an emergent property of the generator map rather than being controlled by construction.

The mapping from forcing variables or generator parameters to drought characteristics is not, in general, monotone or bijective. A space-filling design in the input space does not produce a space-filling design in the hazard space, and the feasible region in drought hazard space is determined by the coupled action of the generator and the drought event extraction procedure. That region is unknown a priori and must be discovered empirically. Figure 1 illustrates the disparity on the Cannonsville inflow: a Latin hypercube design over two Kirsch-Nowak generator decision variables forms a space-filling sample in the parameter space (Figure 1, panel a), and the projection of those same points into the drought hazard space under the generator map is visibly non-uniform, with clumping and gaps that no parameter-space design can control (Figure 1, panel c). No published method searches or subsamples directly in drought hazard space for the purpose of structured coverage across the target drought axes, and this is the gap that the present work addresses.

The first contribution of this paper is a reformulation of drought ensemble generation that produces a structured sample of the feasible drought hazard region in a single optimization run. The method supplies a single anti-ideal point and runs the optimizer once to return an ensemble of synthetic traces whose drought characteristics span the achievable region of the hazard space, so that the grid of attainable target signatures is a byproduct of the run rather than an input to it. This dispenses with the per-target weight retuning and grid enumeration that characterise the predecessor literature. The second contribution is the $L^1$ Manhattan-distance construction that produces this behaviour and the geometric argument that justifies it. The addition of a single Manhattan-distance objective alongside the $K$ target drought characteristics aligns the feasible objective vectors onto a single codimension-one affine subset of the $K+1$-dimensional objective space on which every feasible solution is mutually non-dominated, so that the epsilon-dominance archive of Borg MOEA tiles the set at a spacing determined by the user-supplied epsilon vector. A bijective projection from the objective subset back to the drought characteristic space, bijective on the image of the generator, preserves both dimension and axis orientation. The third contribution is empirical verification of interior-filling coverage across a range of target dimensionalities through a controlled dimension sweep from $K=2$ to $K=6$ on a constrained analytic benchmark, which rules out shell-only and orthant-collapse failure modes of the construction. The fourth contribution is a hazard-space scenario discovery demonstration in which a gradient boosted tree classifier (Friedman, 2001; Chen and Guestrin, 2016) trained on the MOEA-FIND archive is compared with the same classifier trained on an equal-size Latin hypercube subsample of a Kirsch-Nowak library. Scenario discovery in the exploratory-modelling tradition (Bryant and Lempert, 2010; Quinn et al., 2018; Hadjimichael et al., 2020; Quinn et al., 2020; Lau et al., 2023; Bonham et al., 2025) trains classifiers on ensembles of states of the world in parameter or decision-variable coordinates; Section 3.3 performs the same workflow directly in drought hazard coordinates with a binary failure label defined from the drought characteristics themselves, so that the demonstration does not require a downstream policy simulation. The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section 2 defines the drought characterisation pipeline, the structured ensemble generation method, the analytic benchmark problem, the Cannonsville case study, and the coverage and scenario discovery evaluation protocols. Section 3 reports the three empirical results. Section 4 discusses scope, limitations, and extensions. Section 5 concludes.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Quantification of drought characteristics

Drought events in this paper are detected on the Standardized Streamflow Index at three-month accumulation, hereafter SSI-3, following the convention of Zaniolo et al. (2024). For each calendar month, a two-parameter gamma distribution is fit to the historical three-month moving sum of monthly flow and transformed to a standard normal distribution through the inverse normal cumulative distribution function. A drought event begins in the month when SSI-3 first crosses below $-0.84$, which corresponds to the twentieth percentile of the standard normal distribution under the convention adopted by Zaniolo et al. (2024), and ends in the month when SSI-3 first returns above zero. Events of duration less than three months are filtered out, as are events whose minimum SSI-3 value lies above $-1.0$. The extracted event record is the basis for every drought characteristic considered in the remainder of this paper.

Three drought characteristics are used throughout the paper. The first characteristic is the mean drought severity $D_1$, defined as the mean over all detected events of the minimum SSI-3 value within the event, with its sign reversed so that larger values correspond to more severe droughts. The second characteristic is the mean drought duration $D_2$, defined as the mean number of consecutive months within an event during which SSI-3 is below the drought onset threshold of $-0.84$, with units of months. The third characteristic is the mean peak severity month $D_3$, defined as the cyclic mean across events of the calendar month in which the per-event SSI-3 minimum occurs. Cyclic averaging across events uses the arctangent-of-sine-and-cosine estimator that handles the December to January wraparound on the annual calendar, so that the cyclic mean of months 11 and 1 is 12 rather than 6. For a cyclic coordinate on the annual calendar, the absolute-value deviation of a synthesised characteristic from a target value uses the wrapped form $d(m_1, m_2) = \min\bigl( \lvert m_1 - m_2 \rvert, 12 - \lvert m_1 - m_2 \rvert \bigr)$, which remains nonnegative and additive when summed with the non-cyclic characteristics and therefore enters the construction of Section 2.2.3 without modification.

The three-characteristic combination is a concrete choice rather than a prescriptive one. The method developed in Section 2.2 does not inspect the identity of the target drought characteristics, only their real-valued range and the nonnegativity of the per-characteristic absolute deviation from a target value. Analysts can substitute alternative drought features into the same framework. Standard aggregate characteristics from an SSI event detection pipeline, including drought event frequency per decade and cumulative drought deficit, are natural substitutes. Event-level features such as peak intensity per event, maximum single-event cumulative severity, and recovery rate are substitutes when the method is applied to short traces containing one or two drought events. Basin-specific features defined by reservoir operating rules, such as the fraction of months in which an operational drought trigger is active, are substitutes when the vulnerability analysis is policy-specific. The $(D_1, D_2, D_3)$ combination is adopted in this paper because it illustrates both non-cyclic and cyclic characteristics on a standard drought index; the choice is not prescriptive.

### 2.2 Generation of structured drought hazard ensembles

Let $K$ denote the number of target drought characteristic dimensions chosen by the analyst, and let $K+1$ denote the total number of objectives seen by the multi-objective optimizer. The additional objective is a single Manhattan distance to a user-supplied anti-ideal point in drought characteristic space. The notational split between $K$ and $K+1$ is preserved explicitly throughout, so that the $K$ target drought characteristics that define the deliverable ensemble are distinguished from the single auxiliary objective that causes the optimizer to cover them. Let $x \in X$ denote a decision vector drawn from a bounded decision domain $X \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ whose dimensionality $d$ depends on the generator chosen and on the length of the synthetic record to be produced. Let $g: X \to \mathbb{R}^K$ denote the composition of the streamflow generator and the drought event extraction procedure of Section 2.1, so that $g(x) = (D_1(x), \dots, D_K(x))$ is the vector of $K$ drought characteristics produced from the synthetic trace associated with $x$. The map $g$ is well defined but in general nonlinear and many-to-one: distinct decision vectors can produce the same drought characteristic vector. The set $\{D(x) : x \in X\}$ of drought characteristic vectors reachable from any feasible decision vector is the feasible drought hazard region. Let $D^* \in \mathbb{R}^K$ denote a user-supplied anti-ideal point, placed so that $D_j(x) \leq D^*_j$ for every $j$ and every feasible $x$. The deliverable of the method is a structured ensemble $\{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$ of decision vectors whose drought characteristic vectors $\{D(x_1), \dots, D(x_n)\}$ provide coverage of the feasible drought hazard region in the Latin hypercube design-of-experiments sense of McKay et al. (1979), meaning that the sample has the same intrinsic dimensionality as the feasible region and fills the interior of that region rather than concentrating on its boundary. The feasible region is discovered during search and returned as part of the deliverable alongside the ensemble itself.

#### 2.2.1 Kirsch-Nowak synthetic streamflow generator

The nonparametric synthetic streamflow generator adopted in this paper is the block-bootstrap construction of Kirsch et al. (2013), with the multi-site disaggregation extension of Nowak et al. (2010) and the drought-weighted variant of Herman et al. (2016). The algorithm is a monthly resampler. Independent draws from the historical monthly flow record are combined with a Cholesky factorization of the historical twelve-by-twelve monthly correlation matrix, a normal-score transform that imposes the historical correlation structure in Gaussian space, and an inverse normal-score transform back to physical flow units. A shifted water-year construct handles the December to January boundary so that the imposed correlation spans the full calendar year. The construction preserves the historical monthly correlation structure, the flow duration curve, and the seasonal cycle, and has been adopted widely in the exploratory modelling literature for water supply systems (Quinn et al., 2018; Quinn et al., 2019). Three statistics are preserved exactly by algebraic construction regardless of the decision vector supplied by the optimizer: the historical monthly geometric means, through the log-space rescaling and exponentiation of the generator's final step; the monthly log-space standard deviations, through the same rescaling; and the within-year monthly correlation structure, through the Cholesky factorisation of the twelve-by-twelve correlation matrix applied in normal-score space. The December-to-January boundary correlation and the per-month flow duration curve are preserved approximately for finite-length synthetic traces. The inter-annual autocorrelation, the Hurst exponent, and the distribution of annual flow volumes are not fixed by any step in the pipeline and represent the degrees of freedom that MOEA-FIND steers through the decision-variable injection layer of Section 2.2.4. The MOEA-FIND coupling in Section 2.2.4 wraps this generator unchanged; the generator itself is not modified. Parametric alternatives that extend the generated drought envelope beyond the historical record through kappa-distribution marginals and vine-copula dependence (Svensson et al., 2017; Brechmann et al., 2017) are compatible with the MOEA-FIND coupling through the same injection layer and are discussed in Section 4.

#### 2.2.2 Borg MOEA and epsilon-dominance archiving

The multi-objective evolutionary optimizer used by MOEA-FIND is Borg MOEA (Hadka and Reed, 2013), an auto-adaptive many-objective algorithm widely adopted in the water resources optimization literature (Reed et al., 2013; Hadka and Reed, 2015). Three mechanisms of Borg are relevant to the present formulation. The first is the epsilon-dominance external archive, introduced by Laumanns et al. (2002), in which objective space is partitioned into epsilon-boxes of per-axis side length specified by the user and at most one candidate solution is retained per box. The archive delivers controlled cardinality and approximately uniform spacing among retained solutions, so that the search precision is set directly by the analyst's choice of epsilon vector. The second mechanism is epsilon-progress restart triggering: when no new epsilon-box has been populated for a threshold number of function evaluations, the search is restarted from the current archive to escape local optima. The third mechanism is adaptive operator selection across a suite of variational operators (simulated binary crossover, differential evolution, parent-centric crossover, unimodal normal distribution crossover, simplex crossover, polynomial mutation), with operator probabilities updated based on each operator's empirical success at producing offspring that enter the archive. Alternative many-objective algorithms include NSGA-III (Deb and Jain, 2014) and MOEA/D (Zhang and Li, 2007), both of which achieve front coverage through mechanisms that assume the Pareto front has an unknown and potentially irregular geometry in objective space. Section 2.2.3 develops the reason the present formulation is specifically suited to the epsilon-box archive of Borg MOEA rather than to those alternatives.

#### 2.2.3 Manhattan-distance auxiliary objective

This subsection introduces the auxiliary-objective construction that causes Borg MOEA to deliver structured coverage of the full $K$-dimensional feasible drought hazard region along the $K$ orthogonal target axes. The construction aligns the feasible objective vectors with a known $K$-dimensional affine subset of the $K+1$-dimensional objective space, so that the epsilon-dominance archive of Laumanns et al. (2002) as implemented in Borg MOEA by Hadka and Reed (2013) can tile that subset without first needing to discover its shape. A formal development including the periodic case for cyclic drought characteristics is given in Supporting Information 1.

Consider the $K+1$-objective minimization problem
$$
\begin{aligned}
f_j(x) &= D_j(x) \qquad &&j = 1, \dots, K, \\
f_{K+1}(x) &= \sum_{j=1}^{K} \lvert D_j(x) - D^*_j \rvert.
\end{aligned} \tag{1}
$$
All $K+1$ objectives are minimized. The first $K$ objectives are the target drought characteristics themselves, one per drought feature of interest, and minimizing each $f_j$ drives the generator toward the benign end of that drought axis. The $(K+1)$-th objective is the Manhattan distance from the drought characteristic vector of the trace to the anti-ideal point $D^*$. Under the placement $D_j(x) \leq D^*_j$, reducing any individual $D_j$ by an amount $\delta$ increases $f_{K+1}$ by the same $\delta$, so the minimization of $f_j$ and the minimization of $f_{K+1}$ are in exact opposition.

Under the placement $D_j(x) \leq D^*_j$ the absolute value in $f_{K+1}$ reduces to $D^*_j - D_j$, and the sum of all $K+1$ objectives simplifies,
$$
\sum_{j=1}^{K+1} f_j(x) = \sum_{j=1}^{K} D_j(x) + \sum_{j=1}^{K} \bigl( D^*_j - D_j(x) \bigr) = \sum_{j=1}^{K} D^*_j, \tag{2}
$$
which is a constant determined entirely by the anti-ideal. The set of feasible objective vectors therefore lies on the codimension-one affine subset
$$
S = \Bigl\{ f \in \mathbb{R}^{K+1} : \sum_{j=1}^{K+1} f_j = \sum_{j=1}^{K} D^*_j \Bigr\}, \tag{3}
$$
which is a hyperplane of the $K+1$-dimensional objective space in the standard mathematical sense of a codimension-one affine subset. Its intrinsic dimension is $K$, not $K-1$. The word hyperplane is used in this subsection and in Supporting Information 1 exclusively in this sense, and it does not refer to a two-dimensional plane.

The first $K$ coordinates of every objective vector in $S$ are the drought characteristics themselves, and the $(K+1)$-th coordinate is a linear function of those $K$ coordinates under the sum-to-constant identity of equation (2). The map that drops the last coordinate,
$$
\pi: S \to \mathbb{R}^K, \qquad \pi(f_1, \dots, f_K, f_{K+1}) = (f_1, \dots, f_K), \tag{4}
$$
is a bijection between $S$ and the image of the generator map $g$ in drought characteristic space, with the $(K+1)$-th coordinate recovered by $f_{K+1} = \sum_{j=1}^{K} D^*_j - \sum_{j=1}^{K} f_j$. The drought characteristic coordinates are $K$ orthogonal axes by construction, one per target characteristic chosen by the analyst, so the projection preserves both dimensionality and axis orientation. A structured sample of $S$ therefore pulls back to a structured sample of the feasible drought hazard region whose orthogonal axes are the $K$ target characteristics.

Consider two feasible decision vectors $x$ and $y$ whose drought characteristic vectors differ in at least one component, and suppose $f_j(x) < f_j(y)$ for some $j \in \{1, \dots, K\}$. Because both objective vectors lie on $S$ with the same constant sum, there must exist another index $i \in \{1, \dots, K+1\}$ with $f_i(x) > f_i(y)$, so $x$ does not dominate $y$ in the Pareto sense. The argument is symmetric in $x$ and $y$. Every pair of feasible decision vectors whose images under $g$ differ in at least one component is mutually non-dominated. The non-dominance argument holds in the image of $g$, not in the decision space, because $g$ is many-to-one and distinct decision vectors may map to identical drought characteristic vectors. The image of the feasible set is a Pareto-optimal set for the $K+1$-objective minimization problem of equation (1). The epsilon-dominance archive of Borg MOEA retains one representative per epsilon-box of the $K+1$-dimensional objective space that contains at least one feasible objective vector, and under the bijective projection of equation (4) this archive becomes a structured sample of the feasible drought hazard region with spacing inherited from the epsilon vector along each of the $K$ orthogonal target axes.

The anti-ideal placement assumption $D_j(x) \leq D^*_j$ for all feasible $x$ and all $j$ is load-bearing for this construction: if any archive member has $D_j(x) > D^*_j$ in any dimension, the absolute value in $f_{K+1}$ does not reduce to $D^*_j - D_j$, the affine identity of equation (2) fails, the feasible set is no longer confined to $S$, and the non-dominance argument breaks down without any diagnostic signal visible in the optimizer output. For the analytic benchmark, $D^* = (3, 3, \ldots, 3)$ lies strictly outside the $K$-ball by construction and the assumption holds exactly. For the Cannonsville hydrologic application, the assumption requires empirical verification that no archive member exceeds $D^*$ in any drought characteristic dimension, because the Kirsch bootstrap can recombine historical monthly values to produce drought sequences whose statistics exceed those of any single historical event. The protocol for this verification and the response if violations are detected are noted as a Trev-decision in Section 2.4, pending the HPC Phase beta archive.

Figure 3 illustrates the construction in the $K=2$ case. Panel (a) shows the feasible image of the generator map on the codimension-one affine subset $S$ inside the $K+1=3$ objective space, with the anti-ideal point $D^*$ outside the feasible image. Panel (b) shows the bijective projection $\pi$ from $S$ to the $K=2$ drought characteristic plane. Panel (c) shows the epsilon-box lattice tiling of $S$ at side length $\varepsilon$, pulled back through $\pi$ to a structured sample of the feasible drought hazard region. When the feasible drought hazard region is an axis-aligned box in $\mathbb{R}^K$, the construction produces a sample that fills the full box, and the sample axes are the $K$ target characteristics themselves, which is the Latin hypercube limit of McKay et al. (1979). When the feasible region is not axis-aligned, which is typical of drought hazard regions carved out by a nonlinear generator, the construction produces a structured sample of the non-convex feasible subset at intrinsic dimension $K$, matching what a rejection-based Latin hypercube restricted to the same feasible region would produce.

Reference-direction methods such as NSGA-III (Deb and Jain, 2014) and decomposition methods such as MOEA/D (Zhang and Li, 2007) achieve coverage of the Pareto front through user-supplied reference directions or scalarizing subproblems, and both assume the Pareto front has an unknown and potentially irregular geometry in objective space. Borg MOEA, building on the epsilon-dominance archive of Laumanns et al. (2002), retains solutions on a per-box basis in the raw objective space and is geometrically indifferent to the shape of the Pareto front. The construction of this subsection aligns the feasible objective set to a known $K$-dimensional affine subset $S$ and makes the image of the feasible set coincide with the Pareto-optimal set, which is the condition that allows the epsilon-dominance archive to tile the $K$ target drought characteristic axes without any knowledge of their joint feasibility envelope.

#### 2.2.4 Algorithm and implementation

The MOEA-FIND implementation couples Borg MOEA to the nonparametric generator of Section 2.2.1 through a decision-variable wrapper that converts a continuous decision vector produced by the optimizer into the input stream consumed by the generator. Figure 2 shows the algorithmic pipeline. Each Borg decision vector $x \in [0,1]^d$ is converted by the wrapper into either a vector of historical year indices or a vector of standardized residuals, depending on the injection mode. In the index injection mode, each element of $x$ is mapped through the floor function into an integer index that selects a historical year from the monthly flow record. In the residual injection mode, each element of $x$ is mapped through the empirical inverse cumulative distribution function of the historical monthly residuals. In both modes the wrapper then invokes the Kirsch-Nowak pipeline using the converted values in place of the generator's random draws, producing a synthetic monthly flow trace of analyst-specified length. The trace is processed through the drought characterization pipeline of Section 2.1 to produce the drought characteristic vector $D(x)$, and the wrapper returns the $K+1$ objective values of equation (1) to the optimizer.

The main-text results use the residual injection mode because the mapping from $x$ to the generator output is continuous under residual injection and contains integer plateaus under index injection. The continuous map is compatible with the simulated binary crossover and differential evolution variational operators of Borg MOEA, which rely on well-defined local neighborhoods in the decision space. Results for the index injection mode are reported as a sensitivity analysis in Supporting Information 5. Two plausibility constraints are imposed on generated traces to verify that each synthetic trace lies within the statistical envelope of traces the Kirsch-Nowak generator could produce naturally given an infinite ensemble, not to identify drought events. The lag-1 autocorrelation of the trace is required to lie within $0.05$ of the historical lag-1 autocorrelation, and the mean of the non-drought annual flows is required to lie within $15$ percent of the historical average. Both constraints are implemented as soft constraints under the constraint-domination criterion of Deb (2000).

The outer loop of MOEA-FIND is a standard Borg MOEA execution with the $K+1$ objective vector of equation (1). The epsilon-dominance archive grows dynamically during search: there is no fixed population size, and the archive size at termination reflects the number of distinct epsilon-boxes populated by feasible solutions. Population injection for each restart draws from the current archive with a fixed seed. The Cannonsville run uses $K=3$ target drought characteristics with a per-objective epsilon vector derived from a sensitivity sweep: $0.05$ on the normalized severity axis, $0.05$ on the normalized duration axis, $0.5$ months on the peak severity month axis, and $0.05$ on the Manhattan auxiliary objective. The main-text experiments use $100{,}000$ function evaluations per random seed across multiple independent seeds, following the multi-seed replication conventions established by Reed et al. (2013) and Hadka and Reed (2015). Variation operators available to Borg MOEA include simulated binary crossover, differential evolution, parent-centric crossover, unimodal normal distribution crossover, simplex crossover, and polynomial mutation; operator probabilities are updated adaptively based on each operator's empirical success at producing offspring that enter the archive (Hadka and Reed, 2013). All random seeds, configuration parameters, and random number streams are recorded in a per-experiment configuration file archived alongside the output data, and the full figure set is regenerated from the archived output by a single entry-point script.

### 2.3 Analytic benchmark problem

The analytic benchmark used in Section 3.1 is deliberately simple so that the dimension sweep result is not confounded by generator nonlinearity or drought extraction artefacts. The decision space is the bounding box $[-3, 3]^K$ for $K \in \{2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$, and the feasible set is a $K$-dimensional ball of radius $2.5$ centered at the origin, so that a decision vector $x$ is feasible if and only if $\sum_{j=1}^{K} x_j^2 \leq 2.5^2$. The anti-ideal is placed at the positive corner $D^* = (3, 3, \dots, 3)$, which lies outside the $K$-ball for every $K$. The objectives use the same form as equation (1), with $f_j = x_j$ for $j = 1, \dots, K$ and $f_{K+1} = \sum_{j=1}^{K} \lvert x_j - D^*_j \rvert$. Infeasible decision vectors are assigned a penalty objective vector strictly dominated by every feasible objective vector. The $K$-ball is used rather than an axis-aligned sub-box for three reasons. The interior of the $K$-ball is distinct from its boundary in every direction, so a sampler whose archive concentrates on the boundary produces a distance-from-boundary distribution that is visibly different from a sampler that fills the interior. The volume ratio of the $K$-ball inside the bounding box drops rapidly with $K$, providing a stress test of whether the optimizer can still find and tile the feasible interior when rejection cost is high. The $K$-ball is also not axis-aligned, so the construction cannot achieve hypercube-like coverage by accident through axis-aligned sampling. Supporting Information 2 reports the full per-$K$ parameter table including the per-$K$ epsilon vector, which scales from $0.10$ at $K=2$ to $0.30$ at $K=6$.

### 2.4 Delaware River Basin case study

The Cannonsville inflow is the single-site case study for the present work. Cannonsville is one of the three New York City water supply reservoirs in the Delaware River Basin and is the upstream boundary of the Pywr-DRB simulation model used in a companion policy re-evaluation paper. Monthly inflows at Cannonsville (United States Geological Survey gauge 01425000) are drawn from the preprocessed Pywr-DRB dataset, which aggregates daily USGS records over water years 1952 to 2022. The nonparametric generator of Section 2.2.1 is configured with the full-pipeline normal-score transform and the shifted-year December-to-January boundary handling described by Kirsch et al. (2013). All MOEA-FIND runs generate thirty-year monthly traces, matching the trace length used by Borgomeo et al. (2015), Zaniolo et al. (2024), and Wheeler et al. (2025), so that the results reported here are directly comparable to the predecessor literature.

The anti-ideal vector $D^*$ is placed at the historical Cannonsville drought characteristic vector extracted from the same inflow record. Under this placement the optimization explores deviations from the historical drought signature in all three dimensions simultaneously, including the seasonal timing dimension. A library-and-subsample baseline used in Section 3.2 consists of a Kirsch-Nowak library of ten thousand traces generated with independent random seeds. Each library trace is characterized by the same drought features as the MOEA-FIND archive, so that the coverage comparison is conducted between samples of matched size evaluated in the same drought characteristic space. The multi-site extension of the method to multiple Delaware River Basin inflow sites is a scalability note rather than a demonstrated result in the present paper and is deferred to a follow-up, with the relevant protocol summarised in Section 4.

### 2.5 Coverage metrics and evaluation protocols

Coverage quality is evaluated with three metrics drawn from the space-filling design literature. The nearest-neighbor coefficient of variation is the standard deviation of per-point nearest-neighbor distances divided by their mean, so that lower values indicate more uniform spacing among sample points. The $L_2$-star discrepancy is the $L_2$ distance between the empirical distribution of the sample and the uniform distribution on the unit hypercube, as reviewed by Niederreiter (1992), and lower values indicate a more uniform sample. The centered $L_2$ discrepancy is a coordinate-translation-invariant variant of the $L_2$-star discrepancy introduced by Hickernell (1998) and is reported for completeness. These metrics are computed against the unit hypercube and assume a uniform target distribution over that hypercube. When the physically feasible region is a proper subset of the hypercube, which it is in any realistic hydrologic generation problem, the raw metrics reward samplers that place probability mass in infeasible regions at the expense of samplers that concentrate on the achievable subset. A feasible-region-restricted variant used for the Cannonsville comparison in Section 3.2 restricts each metric to the estimated feasible region, defined as the convex hull of the union of all samplers considered. Two additional diagnostics specific to the Manhattan-distance construction are reported alongside the coverage metrics. The interior mass fraction measures the fraction of sample points whose distance from the feasible-region boundary exceeds a fixed radius. The signed orthant occupancy measures the fraction of the $2^K$ signed orthants around the anti-ideal that contain at least one sample point. The reference samplers used in the dimension sweep of Section 3.1 and in the Cannonsville comparison of Section 3.2 are uniform random, Latin hypercube (McKay et al., 1979), and scrambled Sobol (Sobol, 1967; Owen, 1997), each drawn inside the feasible region by rejection at a size matched to the MOEA-FIND archive.

The scenario discovery demonstration of Section 3.3 adapts the exploratory-modelling workflow of Bryant and Lempert (2010), Quinn et al. (2018), Hadjimichael et al. (2020), Quinn et al. (2020), Lau et al. (2023), and Bonham et al. (2025) to drought hazard coordinates rather than parameter coordinates. A binary failure label is defined directly from the drought characteristics of each sample as the joint condition that mean drought severity exceeds the historical 80th percentile and mean drought duration exceeds the historical 80th percentile. The label is therefore a function of the drought characteristics alone and does not require a downstream policy simulation. Two training sets are constructed at matched size. The first is the MOEA-FIND Pareto archive from Section 3.2. The second is an equal-size Latin hypercube subsample of the Kirsch-Nowak library of Section 2.4, drawn in the generator decision variables and projected into the drought hazard space under the generator map. A gradient boosted tree classifier (Friedman, 2001; Chen and Guestrin, 2016) is fit on each training set using the mean drought severity and mean drought duration axes as the scenario discovery coordinate system, with five-fold cross-validation on each training set for hyperparameter selection. Out-of-sample performance is evaluated on a held-out validation set drawn from the full library, reporting the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve, the Brier score, feature importance rankings, and the partial dependence decision boundary in the $(D_1, D_2)$ plane. A logistic regression robustness check using the same two training sets is reported in Supporting Information 6.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Interior coverage on the analytic benchmark problem

The analytic benchmark tests two geometric properties of the MOEA-FIND archive that the hydrologic case cannot test in isolation. The first is whether the affine identity of equation (2) holds to machine precision, confirming that the non-dominance argument of Section 2.2.3 is correctly implemented. The second is whether the epsilon-dominance archive fills the interior of a bounded feasible region rather than concentrating on its boundary or collapsing to a subset of the signed orthants. Both properties must hold before the construction can be applied with confidence to the Kirsch-Nowak generator.

> Preliminary results from EpsNSGAII (platypus) local stand-in runs. The analytic results are valid for the construction because both EpsNSGAII and Borg MOEA use the same epsilon-dominance archive mechanism, which is the only property the theoretical argument requires. All numerical values below will be confirmed with Borg MOEA production runs on HPC.

The affine identity of equation (2) is verified on the unconstrained $K=3$ analytic benchmark. For each of the 682 archive members produced at epsilon $0.15$ and 2,000 function evaluations, the absolute residual $\lvert \sum_{j=1}^{K+1} f_j(x) - \sum_{j=1}^{K} D^*_j \rvert$ is computed. The maximum residual across all archive members is $3.6 \times 10^{-15}$, equal to approximately 16 units in the last place of a double-precision floating-point number near 3, consistent with the rounding error of a four-term sum in IEEE 754 arithmetic. The residual distribution is plotted in Supporting Information Figure SI-1. The machine-precision agreement confirms that every archive member lies on the hyperplane $S$ of equation (3) and that the non-dominance argument holds for the implemented formulation, not merely for the theoretical construction.

Figure 4 reports the interior coverage diagnostics from the dimension sweep at $K \in \{2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$, with 30,000 function evaluations per $K$, seed 42, and the per-$K$ epsilon vector tabulated in Supporting Information 2. Archive sizes are 1,874 at $K=2$, 6,158 at $K=3$, 8,546 at $K=4$, 7,841 at $K=5$, and 7,670 at $K=6$ (Figure 4, panel a); the non-monotone peak at $K=4$ reflects the interaction between the epsilon-box resolution and the shrinking feasible volume fraction at higher $K$. The mean Manhattan distance from the anti-ideal at $K=3$ is 8.98 for MOEA-FIND against 8.95 for uniform random, 9.04 for Latin hypercube, and 9.00 for Sobol (Figure 4, panel b), all within one percent of one another and consistent with sampling variability across realisations of matched size in a ball of radius 2.5. The interior mass fraction at $K=3$, defined as the fraction of archive members whose distance from the $K$-ball boundary exceeds 0.25 in the unit-radius normalisation, is 0.731 for MOEA-FIND against 0.728 for uniform, 0.728 for Latin hypercube, and 0.725 for Sobol (Figure 4, panel c). At $K=6$, where the ball volume is a small fraction of the bounding box volume, the interior mass fraction rises to 0.648 for MOEA-FIND against 0.540 for uniform, 0.531 for Latin hypercube, and 0.529 for Sobol. The MOEA-FIND archive concentrates more probability mass in the feasible interior at high dimension than any of the three reference samplers at every tested $K$, which is the defining property of interior-filling rather than boundary-concentrating coverage. The signed orthant occupancy is 1.00 at every $K$ from 2 to 6, confirming that the archive populates all $2^K$ signed orthants (Figure 4, panel d), including all 64 orthants at $K=6$. The shell-only and orthant-collapse failure modes that a conservative reading of the Manhattan-distance construction would predict are both refuted empirically at every tested dimensionality.

The fine-scale grid cell occupancy diagnostic, reported in Supporting Information 2, shows that the MOEA-FIND archive populates 68 percent of the feasible grid cells at $K=6$ on a six-bin-per-axis partition, against 82 percent for uniform random, 82 percent for Latin hypercube, and 88 percent for Sobol. Relative to the uniform reference, the MOEA-FIND archive covers approximately 84 percent as many feasible cells. Coverage at $K \leq 5$ is comparable across all samplers. The grid occupancy gap at $K=6$ is interior rather than boundary-based, as established by the elevated interior mass fraction reported above, and is attributable to epsilon-dominance clustering at the resolution set by the epsilon vector of 0.30 and the function evaluation budget of 30,000. The gap can be reduced by lowering the epsilon vector or increasing the function evaluation budget. The $K=6$ deviation is therefore a finite-resolution artefact at the tested parameter setting rather than a geometric failure of the construction.

## 4. Discussion

MOEA-FIND is a structured sampler of the feasible drought hazard region rather than a replacement for quasi-Monte Carlo samplers on an unconstrained box. The method is appropriate when the drought hazard region is unknown a priori, when the generator map from decision variables to drought characteristics is nonlinear enough that input-space space-filling designs do not produce hazard-space space-filling samples, and when the downstream analysis requires coverage in the drought hazard coordinates rather than in forcing-variable coordinates. The parameter-space versus hazard-space contrast introduced in Figure 1 is the epistemic shift that the method delivers. In the taxonomy of Herman et al. (2015) and the decision support process of Moallemi et al. (2020), scenario generation constitutes a distinct methodological stage — Axis II (States of the World) in Herman's four-axis taxonomy and Stage II fork 3.2.2 (Framing future scenarios) in Moallemi's decision support framework. Prior methods at this stage sample the parameter uncertainty space: design of experiments, stress-testing, and many-objective robust optimisation all draw parameter values from the uncertainty space and forward-propagate through the generator map to the outcome space. Moallemi et al. (2020) describe the result of this forward propagation as defining three distinct spaces (a decision space of candidate policies, an uncertainty space of states of the world, and an outcome space of performance measures), where the standard workflow samples the first two and measures the third. MOEA-FIND operates in a fourth mode at this same stage: the sample is structured directly in the hazard coordinates of the outcome space at generation time rather than inferred from a sample structured upstream in the parameter space.

The method does not apply uniformly across problem types. When the feasible drought hazard region is already known, convex, and axis-aligned, a rejection-based Latin hypercube sample restricted to that region is simpler and achieves the same coverage outcome without a multi-objective optimizer. When the number of target drought characteristics exceeds approximately six, the number of function evaluations required to tile the $K+1$-dimensional objective space at a useful epsilon vector grows rapidly, and the dimension sweep of Section 3.1 shows fine-scale clustering beginning at $K=6$ at the function evaluation budget used there; the clustering can be mitigated by reducing the epsilon vector or by increasing the function evaluation budget at additional computational cost. When the generator is very slow per evaluation, as is sometimes the case for coupled surface-subsurface models, the required function evaluation budget becomes prohibitive, and a library-and-subsample alternative is the practical fallback. When the analyst requires drought signatures that lie in the deep tail beyond the envelope of the historical record, the nonparametric Kirsch-Nowak generator cannot produce them, and the MOEA-FIND archive will reveal the infeasibility by failing to populate those regions of the drought hazard space. In that situation a parametric generator with extrapolation capability is required. Finally, the non-dominance argument of Section 2.2.3 depends on the Manhattan distance, and the use of Euclidean or Chebyshev distances in place of the Manhattan distance does not yield the affine identity of equation (2), so a different distance metric requires a different coverage argument and should be validated numerically on a constrained benchmark before deployment.

The ability of MOEA-FIND to accept user-defined drought characteristics follows from the Manhattan-distance construction and not from any case-specific choice of feature set. Any real-valued drought feature for which the sum of absolute deviations from an anti-ideal value is well defined is a valid substitute for the $(D_1, D_2, D_3)$ triple used in the Cannonsville case study. Cyclic calendar features such as the mean peak severity month use the wrapped distance of Section 2.1 and inherit the nonnegativity and additivity properties the affine identity of equation (2) relies on. Event-level features such as peak intensity per event, maximum cumulative severity, and recovery rate are natural substitutes when the method is applied to short traces containing one or two drought events, which is a direct extension of the framework to the event-level formulation used in the climate stress-testing literature. Basin-specific features defined by operating rules of the water system, such as the fraction of months in which a drought-stage trigger is active, can also be substituted when the vulnerability analysis is policy-specific; the method does not inspect the identity of the drought characteristics, only their real-valued range and the nonnegativity of the per-characteristic absolute deviation from a target value.

The extension of MOEA-FIND to a parametric streamflow generator with kappa-distribution marginals and vine-copula temporal dependence (Svensson et al., 2017; Brechmann et al., 2017) is a direct substitution at the wrapper level of Figure 2 and leaves the Manhattan-distance construction of Section 2.2.3 unchanged. The parametric extension would allow generation of drought signatures beyond the envelope of the historical record, which the nonparametric Kirsch-Nowak generator cannot. The multi-site extension of the method to multiple inflow sites in a single basin is likewise a wrapper-level substitution; shared bootstrap indices across sites preserve cross-site drought dependence as a natural consequence of the Kirsch-Nowak pipeline (Nowak et al., 2010) rather than requiring cross-site correlation to be placed in the objective vector as done by Wheeler et al. (2025). Both extensions are the subject of follow-up work.

The implications of MOEA-FIND for robust decision making and vulnerability analysis are twofold. The archive is a ready-made input ensemble for the MORDM workflow of Kasprzyk et al. (2013), with the property that the drought characteristic coverage of the ensemble is controlled by construction rather than left as an emergent property of input-space sampling. Hadjimichael et al. (2024) observe that the consequential scenarios identified by scenario discovery workflows depend sensitively on the way the analyst aggregates performance across states of the world, and hazard-space sampling at generation time addresses this sensitivity by structuring the ensemble in the drought characteristic coordinates in which failure labels are defined rather than leaving that structure to emerge from upstream aggregation choices. The gradient boosted tree scenario discovery exercise of Section 3.3 demonstrates that the downstream vulnerability analysis workflow used throughout the exploratory modelling literature (Bryant and Lempert, 2010; Hadjimichael et al., 2020; Quinn et al., 2020; Lau et al., 2023; Bonham et al., 2025) can be conducted directly in drought hazard space on a MOEA-FIND archive. The companion Delaware River Basin policy re-evaluation paper uses the MOEA-FIND archive as the input ensemble for a Pywr-DRB simulation and reports the downstream policy implications of the archive for the New York City water supply operating rules.
